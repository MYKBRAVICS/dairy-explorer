Section 01 — Foundation

Introduction & Enterprise Overview

Dairy production integrates precision nutrition, reproductive efficiency, udder health, genetics, and financial management into one of agriculture's most demanding and rewarding enterprises.

Production Systems
🏭 Confinement (TMR-based)
  • Total Mixed Ration fed in freestall or drylot barns
  • High production per cow (10,000–14,000+ kg/year)
  • Maximum control over nutrition and environment
  • High capital investment; high labor efficiency at scale
  • Common in North America, Europe, Israel
🌿 Pasture-Based / Seasonal
  • Grazing provides majority of diet (>60% of DM intake)
  • Lower production (6,000–9,000 kg/year) but lower costs
  • Seasonal calving around pasture growth
  • Premium markets: organic, grass-fed, A2
  • Common in NZ, Ireland, Australia, East Africa

Section 02 — Life Cycle

The Dairy Cow Life Cycle

Understanding the complete lactation cycle is essential for matching nutritional supply to physiological demand. Each stage has distinct metabolic priorities and ration requirements.

Life Cycle Timeline
🍼
Birth → 8 Weeks
Calf Phase: Colostrum, Milk & Starter
Critical immune window (0–24 hrs). Colostrum feeding, milk/milk replacer, gradual starter introduction. Weaning at 6–8 weeks. Target: 0.6–0.8 kg ADG.
🌱
8 Weeks → 6 Months
Weanling / Pre-Weaning Heifer
High-growth forage + grain diet. Target 0.8–1.0 kg/day ADG. Rumen development critical. Vaccinations and parasite control.
🐄
6 Months → First Breeding (14–15 months)
Growing Heifer
Moderate growth: 0.75–0.85 kg/day. Must reach 55–60% of mature body weight at breeding. Avoid over-conditioning (BCS > 3.5).
🤰
Bred → First Calving (~24 months)
Pregnant Heifer / Springing Heifer
Target 85–90% of mature body weight at first calving. Transition nutrition begins 3 weeks pre-calving. BCS 3.25–3.5 at calving.
🌅
Calving → Day 21
Fresh Cow / Early Transition
Highest metabolic stress period. Negative energy balance. Monitor for metabolic diseases (ketosis, milk fever, displaced abomasum). Aggressive monitoring critical.
🥛
Day 21 → Day 100 (Peak)
Early Lactation / Peak Milk
Maximum milk yield reached ~6–8 weeks post-calving. Cow in NEB. Rebreed target: conception by Day 80–85. Feed intake still catching up to production demands.
📈
Day 100 → Day 200 (Mid Lactation)
Mid Lactation / Recovery Phase
Milk gradually declining. Cow in positive energy balance — rebuilding body reserves. Pregnancy progressing. Optimize BCS to 3.0–3.25.
🌿
Day 200 → Dry-Off (~Day 305)
Late Lactation / Pre-Dry
Reduce energy density. Target BCS 3.25–3.5 at dry-off. Avoid over-conditioning. Prepare for dry period intramammary therapy.
🌛
Dry-Off → 3 Weeks Pre-Calving (45–60 days)
Far-Off Dry Period
Low-energy, high-fiber diet. Maintain BCS — not gain. Mammary gland regeneration. Colostrum development begins.
⚡
3 Weeks Pre-Calving → Calving
Close-Up / Transition Period
DCAD nutrition (anionic diet), Vitamin E & Se loading, rumen adaptation to lactation diet. Highest-impact management period in the entire lactation cycle.

Milking Cow Nutrition & Rations

The milking herd is the revenue engine of the dairy. Rations must support peak production, maintain body condition, sustain reproduction, and preserve cow health.

💡
TMR Formulation Principle

Total Mixed Rations blend all forages, concentrates, and supplements into a single homogeneous mix. This prevents sorting, ensures every bite meets nutrient targets, and supports stable rumen pH. NDF from forage is the foundation — never below 19% of DM from forage sources.

Dry Cow Nutrition 🌿
Section 04 — Dry Cow

Far-Off Dry Cow Nutrition (Days 1–42)

The dry period allows mammary gland regeneration and nutrient replenishment. Controlled energy intake is critical to avoid over-conditioning.

⚠ Dry Cow Principle: Prevent Over-Conditioning

Target BCS 3.25–3.5 at dry-off. Cows gaining BCS during the dry period experience increased risk of retained placenta, displaced abomasum, ketosis, and poor reproductive performance. Low-energy, high-NDF rations are the solution.

Close-Up / Transition ⚡
Section 05 — Transition Nutrition

Close-Up / Transition Cow Rations (Days −21 to Calving)

The transition period is the highest-impact management phase in the dairy. Objective: adapt the rumen, prevent hypocalcemia, and load antioxidants to reduce metabolic disease risk.

⚡
DCAD Nutrition: The Cornerstone of Transition Management

DCAD = (Na + K) − (Cl + S), measured in mEq/kg DM. A negative DCAD (−100 to −150 mEq/kg DM) induces mild compensated metabolic acidosis, activating the Ca-mobilization system before calving. Target urine pH of 6.0–7.0 in cows on anionic diets.

Section 08 — Young Calves

Calf Nutrition: Birth to Weaning (0–8 Weeks)

The neonatal period is the most critical and highest-mortality risk phase. Colostrum management within the first 6 hours of life is the single most important action a dairy manager can take.

The Colostrum Golden Rule — First 6 Hours

Feed a minimum of 3–4 liters of first-milking colostrum within 2 hours of birth, and a second feeding of 2 liters within 6–12 hours. IgG absorption peaks at birth and drops to near-zero by 24 hours. Measure colostrum quality with a Brix refractometer — Brix ≥22% = good quality.

Calf Starter & Hay Program
🌽 Calf Starter (Weeks 1–8)
  • High-energy, palatable, pelleted or texturized starter — 20–22% CP, 70%+ TDN
  • Introduce from Day 3–4; ad libitum access from Day 7 onward
  • Typical ingredients: ground corn, oats, soybean meal, molasses, mineral premix
  • Ensure fresh water access from Week 1 — critical for starter intake
  • Weaning criterion: consuming ≥1.0 kg starter/day for 3 consecutive days
🌿 Hay Introduction (Weeks 3–8)
  • Introduce good-quality grass or mixed hay from Week 3–4
  • Small amounts initially: 50–100 g/day, increasing to 200–400 g by weaning
  • Hay stimulates rumen development and prevents papillae fusion
  • Avoid legume-only hay early (risk of bloat in pre-weaned calves)
  • Chop to 3–5 cm for young calves to encourage intake

⚠
Over-Conditioning Warning: Growing Heifers

Heifers gaining >1.0 kg/day consistently develop excess mammary fat (adipose infiltration of mammary gland), which permanently reduces lifetime milk yield by 10–15%. Target BCS 3.0–3.25 at first breeding; avoid starchy, energy-dense rations in 3–12 month heifers.

Bull Key Nutrients for Semen Quality

Zinc (65–80 mg/kg DM): Testosterone synthesis; sperm motility. Selenium + Vitamin E: Spermatozoa membrane integrity. Vitamin A: Sperm production in seminiferous tubules. Energy balance: Over-conditioned bulls (BCS >4) have reduced libido and semen quality — keep bulls lean and active.

Section 11 — Reproduction

Reproductive Management & Protocols

Every day open beyond 85 days costs approximately $3–6 in lost income. A 365-day calving interval is the gold standard.

Voluntary Waiting Period & Breeding Strategy
  • Voluntary Waiting Period (VWP): 50–60 days post-calving. Allows uterine involution and metabolic recovery before first insemination. High-producing herds may extend to 70 days.
  • First Service AI: Observe for estrus using tail paint, activity monitors (Heatime, AfiMilk), or visual observation. Breed 12–18 hours after standing heat.
  • Pregnancy Detection: Rectal palpation at 35–45 days; ultrasonography at 25–32 days. Recheck at 60–90 days.
  • Days in Milk to Conception Target: Less than 115 days. Days open exceeding 150 should trigger a reproductive review.

Lameness Prevention & Hoof Care
  • Hoof trimming: Functional trimming at dry-off and 100–150 DIM. Professional trimmer every 6 months minimum.
  • Footbath program: Copper sulfate (5%) or formalin (3–5%) footbath 4× per week. Monitor for digital dermatitis — the most prevalent lameness cause globally.
  • Flooring: Rubber mats or grooved concrete. Stocking density <120% on freestall beds. Wet floors increase lameness 40–60%.
  • Nutrition-lameness link: Biotin (20 mg/day), zinc methionine, and selenium improve hoof horn quality. Subacute rumen acidosis (SARA) is a primary cause of laminitis.
  • Body condition score: Thin cows (BCS <2.5) have poor digital cushion; obese cows (BCS >4.0) at calving have high laminitis risk.
Mastitis Control Program (5-Point Plan)
  1. Teat disinfection at milking: Pre-dipping with iodine or chlorhexidine (30-second contact time); post-dipping with barrier teat dip after every milking.
  2. Milking hygiene: Clean, dry udder preparation. Single-use paper towels. Consistent milking routine reducing letdown time.
  3. Dry cow therapy: Antibiotic infusion at dry-off + internal teat sealant. Cures 60–80% of existing IMI.
  4. Cull chronic cows: Cows with SCC >400,000 cells/mL for 3+ consecutive months or 3+ clinical mastitis cases per lactation are economically unviable.
  5. Milking machine maintenance: Annual servicing. Vacuum level 38–44 kPa; pulsation ratio 60:40; liner replacement every 2,500 milkings or 3 months.

Facilities & Housing 🏗
Section 14 — Facilities

Facilities, Housing & Environment

Cow comfort is not a luxury — it is a direct production input. Cows that lie down for 12+ hours per day produce 2–3 kg more milk and have better reproductive performance.

🏠 Freestall Barn Design Standards
  • Stall width: 1.2–1.3 m for Holsteins; 1.0–1.15 m for Jerseys
  • Stall length: 2.4–2.6 m (head-to-wall); 2.2–2.4 m (nose-to-nose)
  • Stall surface: mattress + 1–2 kg dry bedding/day; or deep sand (best udder health)
  • Feed alley width: 3.5–4.5 m; feed bunk space: 0.6–0.7 m/cow
  • Water trough space: ≥10 cm/cow; ≥2 water points per pen
  • Stocking density: 100–110% of stall number; >120% increases stress significantly
  • Ventilation: ≥60 air changes/hour in summer; tunnel ventilation + fans in heat stress zones
  • Heat abatement: fans (2.4–3.0 m diameter) + sprinklers above feed bunk; target THI < 72
🏗 Milking Parlor Types
  • Herringbone: 50–350 cows; labor-efficient; most common worldwide
  • Parallel: Faster milking; higher throughput; minimal cow interaction during attachment
  • Rotary (internal/external): 500–5,000+ cows; high automation; requires skilled operators
  • Automatic Milking Systems (AMS/Robots): Self-directed milking; 60–70 cows/unit; high capital; improved cow welfare; real-time data
  • Milk-out interval: 8–12 hours; robots: 2–4× daily voluntary; 3× milking adds 8–15% milk
  • Milking duration: 4–8 minutes total; avoid over-milking

Profit Maximization Strategies
📈 Revenue Enhancement
  • Maximize milk components (fat & protein) through precision nutrition — component pricing pays premium
  • 3× milking vs 2×: adds 8–15% production; evaluate labor cost vs revenue gain
  • Extend productive life: each additional lactation reduces heifer replacement cost by $400–600
  • Produce value-added milk: organic, A2, grass-fed — 15–50% price premiums
  • Cull cow marketing: time slaughter for best market prices; fat culls add $50–200/head
  • Milk solids contracts: negotiate milk price based on kg fat + protein, not volume
💰 Cost Reduction
  • Grow forages on-farm: corn silage at $50–90/ton vs. $130–200/ton purchased
  • Reduce heifer replacement cost through improved calf health (target <5% mortality)
  • Dry cow optimization: selective dry therapy cuts antibiotic costs 30–50%
  • Reduce SCC: every 100,000-cell reduction improves yield 1–2% and milk price by $0.02–0.05/L
  • Benchmark feed efficiency: target 1.4–1.6 kg milk/kg DM; use TMR software
  • Manure value: anaerobic digestion for biogas; composted manure = $15–30/ton fertilizer value

Section 16 — Sustainability

Sustainability, Technology & Future Strategies

Top-performing dairies combine elite genetics, precision nutrition, rigorous transition cow management, and a culture of continuous improvement through data.

Precision Dairy Technologies
  • Automatic Milking Systems (AMS): Real-time milk yield, conductivity, color, and fat/protein per quarter per cow per milking. Early mastitis detection and cow behavioral data.
  • Activity monitors / rumination boluses: Detect heat, illness, rumen dysfunction. Heatime, SCR, and similar systems improve conception rates 15–25% over visual heat detection.
  • In-line milk analyzers: Real-time fat, protein, lactose, SCC per cow. Enables daily ration adjustments and early disease detection.
  • TMR audit software: Ensures TMR accuracy — deviation from target >5% in key ingredients significantly impacts production and health.
  • Drone-based and satellite forage assessment: NDVI mapping of pastures; identifies fertility needs; tracks grazing allocation.
  • Genomic testing: SNP chips (50K, 70K) provide breeding values for all traits at birth with accuracy equivalent to a daughter-proven bull. Enables faster genetic progress.
